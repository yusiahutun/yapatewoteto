'He's spending more on his baldness than on combating malaria': Bill Gates' scathing attack on Berlusconi's vanity

Microsoft founder Bill Gates has launched a scathing attack on Silvio Berlusconi, accusing the Italian Prime Minister of spending more on his thinning hair than he does on foreign aid.

Billionaire Mr Gates hit out at Mr Berlusconi's 'stinginess' and said the controversial politican was the main figure on his 'list of shame'.

And, in a clear reference to Mr Berlusconi's hair transplant, he told German daily Sueddeutche Zeitung that 'rich people spend a lot more on personal problems like baldness than they do to combat malaria'.

Mr Gates' attack came as Italians were scratching their heads over the mystery of Mr Berlusconi's moving hairline.

Changing crown: Italian PM Silvio Berlusconi sported a full head of hair (left) on January 23. Two days later (centre) his hairline appeared to have receeded, while on January 27 his follicles had grown back again

Scathing: Microsoft founder Bill Gates at the World Economic Forum in Davos, Switzerland yesterday

Photographs taken recently of the 73-year-old prime minister seem to show him with a good head of hair one day but then visibly thinner a couple of days later.

A further two days on and his thatch has mysteriously reappeared.

The media tycoon had a hair transplant in 2004 - days before he was pictured with Tony and Cherie Blair when they holidayed at his Sardinian villa.

Much to the embarrassment of the Blairs, Mr Berlusconi appeared with them wearing a black and white bandanna to protect his scalp from the sun.

Yesterday Italian newspapers printed a series of photos showing Mr Berlusconi's changing hairline. La Repubblica said his hair had 'undergone an amazing mutation'.

It added that after appearing at a wedding 'with his traditional head of hair', two days later his hairline 'appeared inexplicably higher and thinner. But have no fear: At a ceremony on Wednesday it was back to its luxuriant thick foliage.'

Last night Dr Piero Rosati, who performed Mr Berlusconi's eight hour hair transplant, said: 'It's possible that hair can fall out due to stress but more hair or less hair what is important is what he is doing politically.

Mr Berlusconi is greeted by supporters as he arrives at Reggio Calabria Prefecture for a meeting today

'I have not seen him since the operation as after the transplant there is usually no need to do so - once the hair takes the patient can go to the hairdresser, comb it and do anything they like.

'It's very unusual for hair to fall out after a transplant - I have done this job for 28 years so I should know but without seeing him personally it is difficult to say if there is a problem.'

However Professor Franco Buttafaro, a specialist in plastic surgery, said the problem was down to a far simpler set of circumstances - Mr Berlusconi had forgotten to put his make up on.

Professor Buttafaro told Italian glossy magazine Novella 2000: 'Quite simply there is nothing dramatic here - it just the fact that he has not applied his make up properly to the scalp.

'In two days he would not have lost his hair to stress - looking at the photos, what has happened is that one day he applied the make up correctly the next day he didn't.

'The cream is used to blend in with the hair and then spread with silicone which creates a volumizing effect and make sit all compact and shiny. I think he also uses a pencil to add the finishing touches to the join.'

Bandanna Berlusconi: The Italian PM meets Tony and Cherie Blair in 2004, just days after he famously had a hair transplant

Professor Buttafaro then blamed the original transplant and said: 'The hairs were inserted to far apart, they were 2-3mm away from each other, that's far too excessive.

'In effect in a square centimetre he has maybe 25 hairs when he should have at least 70.'

One Italian commentator said: 'Berlusconi takes great pride in his public appearance and we know he has had a hair transplant, wears powder and always has a year round tan.

'The photos are quite striking. In one he does look particularly pale and the hairline does appear noticeably higher, yet two days later he is looking fit and well and back with a full crown of hair.'

One report suggested that stress may have led to his hair falling out.

In December he was hit in the face by an attacker wielding a statue, he spent much of last year fighting off allegations involving models and escort girls, his wife is said to be demanding a £40million divorce, and he is facing several corruption trials.

Last night his spokesman refused to comment on the stories about his hair.
