How to buy a Victorian engagement ring

February 11, 2024
blog

While divorce rings may have stolen the limelight recently, engagement rings are once more making headlines. According to Pinterest’s 2024 wedding report, antique Victorian rings are majorly trending for this year’s brides-to-be. Google Trends backs their findings – in the last three months, searches for ‘Victorian engagement rings’ have grown by 120 per cent. “Picking out an engagement or wedding ring comes with a lot of considerations, but for couples in 2024 one of the biggest concerns is balancing sustainability with aesthetics and meaning,” explains Hamish Shephard, founder of Bridebook. “That’s where Victorian rings come in – in an era where sustainability is at the forefront of couples’ minds, their resurgence offers the chance to blend timeless elegance and symbolism with ethical sourcing.”

Where to begin your search? Auction houses and expert antique jewellers are the best place to start if you want an authentic original, but it’s important to know what you’re looking for. “Victorian rings have distinctive features that make them a beautiful choice of ring over 100 years since their inception,” explains Charlotte Sayers, a Victorian jewellery expert who sells her pieces at London’s Grays Antiques Market in Mayfair. “Rings often feature intricate gold work crafted from 18-karat gold or a mix of 18-karat gold and silver, and chunky coloured gemstones were also popular, such as rubies, sapphires, and emeralds.”

When it comes to coloured gems, it’s important to check with your jeweller that any coloured stones are unheated and untreated, and if your budget is tight then it may help to consider rings that feature collections of smaller stones, which will be much more affordable than ultra-large solitaires. If you’re unsure on the style you prefer, research historic pieces and notable Victorian engagement rings in recent years – Katy Perry’s Victorian ruby and diamond ring features a floral setting which epitomises contemporary classicism.

Be on the lookout for meaningful messages, too; symbolism was very important in Victorian jewellery, and these emblems still appeal to modern brides who want something a little unique. “Victorian pieces often conveyed romantic messages through motifs such as hearts, flowers, and snakes, which represented everlasting love,” says Sayer. “Queen Victoria’s engagement ring was famously a snake set with an emerald, ruby and a diamond,” she adds.

John Josephs, another of Grays Antiques Market’s specialists who deals in rare jewellery circa 1840 to 1970, cites three-stone Victorian rings to be a popular choice, but quality is key. “The settings are very important, the piece should be in good condition, and the stones must be of a decent quality,” he explains. You may, however, be surprised to hear that hallmarking isn’t a reliable guide to a ring’s authenticity. “A lot of Victorian rings aren’t hallmarked, and the ones with hallmarks are more modern. It is the Victorian rings with pearls that do have hallmarks, but I would not advise them as engagement rings,” he warns, referencing the delicate nature of pearls and their inability to withstand everyday wear and tear. “If a ring is hallmarked, it will have the town, date letter and karat of the gold, but many rings may just be stamped 18ct,” adds Sayers. “Just be sure to buy from a reputable source that guarantees the piece.”

When it comes to durability, a Victorian diamond ring is a safe choice, but be sure to establish whether it features a modern or antique stone. "Diamonds from the Victorian era are classified as old cut diamonds,” explains Guy Hancock, managing director of Hancocks London, an antique jeweller in Mayfair’s Burlington Arcade that specialises in such gems. “These stones are unique and offer a one-of-a-kind romance that you can only find with an antique diamond,” he says. “The most exceptional ones that we select for our collection are wonderfully lively, originally created and cut when proportions were not dictated by exact percentages, but to create the maximum beauty from that individual stone, and to be admired by candlelight.”

If you’re too impatient to track down the perfect original, there are modern designers who echo Victorian techniques to achieve vintage-feel pieces inspired by the period’s design codes. Jessica McCormack’s three-stone trilogy ring is a striking example, with her signature button-back setting, while sapphire and diamond cluster designs by Garrard and Amrapali subtly hark back to Victorian posy styles. Whether original, or antique-inspired, it should be easy to find a piece that speaks to you, and one that will stand out in a sea of modern solitaires.
