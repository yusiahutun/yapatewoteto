Groundbreaking Artist Faith Ringgold Has Died

March 31, 2024
blog
John Edmonds

Faith Ringgold, photographed for Harper’s Bazaar by John Edmonds in 2021

Faith Ringgold, whose powerful quilt paintings and mixed-media works stitched together a rich patchwork of intricate—and often provocative—narratives around the subjects of race, gender, and Black life in America, has died at the age of 93.

Born and raised in Harlem, Ringgold started out teaching art in the New York City public schools during the 1950s and the 1960s amid the swirl of the Civil Rights movement at a time when Black artists—and women in particular—were all but excluded from most mainstream galleries and institutions. Her response was to embark on creating her own body of work, at once intensely personal and brazenly political, tackling topics like motherhood, family, and community against the backdrop of the legacies of systemic discrimination, hate, and violence still very much alive in a fractured country.

By then Ringgold was a single mother of two daughters, Barbara and Michele, from her marriage to pianist Robert Earl Wallace, and was working to balance her personal and professional responsibilities with her creative ambitions. Not long after, she married Burdette Ringgold and began to pursue art-making in earnest. “My work was about the problems of living and being a Black woman in that era; of being a mother, which I was, and a schoolteacher, which I had been; of being an artist who was all of these things,” Ringgold told Bazaar in 2022, on the eve of a blockbuster retrospective, “Faith Ringgold: American People,” at New York’s New Museum. “It was really about me and what I was having to go through to be who I wanted to be. It was about me telling my story. I always thought it was important for people to tell their stories.”

“Those skills were like a form of inheritance.”

“American People” took its title from one of Ringgold’s best-known series of quilt paintings, which graphically chronicled the trauma and bloodshed that she and so many other Americans were witnessing and experiencing amid the struggle for equality. A centerpiece of the cycle, American People Series #20: Die (1967), inspired in part by Pablo Picasso’s famous anti-war painting Guernica (1937), is a two-panel tableau depicting an eerie scene of carnage, death, and chaos, punctuated by two children clinging desperately to one another in fear.

The content of Ringgold’s work could be trenchant and even incendiary, but sometimes stood in contrast to its form. The practice of quilting, she told Bazaar, was one she had learned from her mother and part of a family tradition the went back to her enslaved great-great-grandmother. “To me, those skills were like a form of inheritance,” she said. “When slavery began, these women who came from Africa could use quilt-making as a way to do art, and it was okay because their owners didn’t think of it as art-making. A quilt was something you put on top of yourself to keep you warm; it wasn’t art. But to these women, the quilts were almost like letters, a form of communication. I loved the idea that they’d developed this art-making system from their imagination, that they were able to create something beautiful out of scraps.”

“For me, art has always been about seeing.”

Throughout the 1960s and 1970s, Ringgold participated in numerous actions and demonstrations. She was arrested on numerous occasions for staging protests of art-world institutions that had historically and systemically excluded women and artists of color, including the Museum of Modern Art, the Metropolitan Museum of Art, and Whitney Museum of American Art—all of which now have her work in their permanent collections. (MoMA acquired American People Series #20: Die in 2016.)

Ringgold was also a successful author, writing and illustrating books for children like Tar Beach (1991) and Aunt Harriet’s Railroad in the Sky (1992), which approached many of the same themes she dealt with in her art from an entirely new perspective.

Acceptance in the art world, though, did not come easily or quickly for her. She was already in her mid-50s when the Studio Museum in Harlem mounted the first major institutional survey of her work in 1984, which is part of what made the explosion of interest in her art over the past decade not just gratifying but important to her. “As for my own legacy? I hope my work expresses the life and deeds of Black people and other aspects of the world that have thrilled me,” Ringgold told Bazaar. “For me, art has always been about seeing. In a way, though, it can also inspire you to dream up what you don’t see—the things you imagine and can make real as a work of art.”
