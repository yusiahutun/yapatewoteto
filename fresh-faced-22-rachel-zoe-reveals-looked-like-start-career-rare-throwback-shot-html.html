Rachel Zoe has revealed what she looked like as a fresh-faced stylist at the tender age of 22.

Giving out career advice as part of LinkedIn's new series If I Were 22, the now 42-year-old breaks down the six pieces of advice she would focus if she 'could go back and do it all over.'

Accompany the nostalgic essay is an image of Ms Zoe when she was just starting out in her career, at her first job in New York as a fashion assistant at the now-defunct YM magazine (her salary: $18,000).

Rachel Zoe has revealed what she looked like at the tender age of 22, when she was just starting out in her career in her first job in New York as a fashion assistant at the now-defunct YM magazine

'It was through a combination of instinct and great advice from my parents—for which I am eternally grateful—that I was able to navigate the uncharted territory of the fashion world,' the mother-of-two writes.

Her top tips for success? 'Don’t look at the clock, learn what you’re good and what you’re not good at, know what to prioritize, rise above drama, position yourself accordingly and stand out in an interview.'

During her first job at YM magazine, Ms Zoe said she stayed as late as she could and was always the first person to work in the morning - which she believes made her stand out.

Giving out career advice as part of LinkedIn's new editorial series If I Were 22, the now 42-year-old breaks down the six pieces of advice she would focus if she 'could go back and do it all over'

'This is obviously easier to do if you love your job, but even if you are in an assistant or entry-level role, you must understand that putting in the extra time will move you closer to what you want to do,' she admits.

Acknowledging weaknesses is also a 'key component' to success, she believes.

'When you identify your shortcomings, you are empowered to ask for help and pair up with others who will lend a hand while also benefiting from your strengths.'

With two small children - Skyler, three, and Kaius, six months -learning how to prioritize has been a major part of her career success, she says

She explains that her business-savvy husband, Roger Berman, is the yin to her yang. 'I bring to the table a more creative outlook and gut instinct,' she says.

With two small children - Skyler, three, and Kaius, six months -prioritizing is also important.

'With several different facets to my business—designing, The Zoe Report, the styling studio and being a mother —I have had no choice but to learn how to prioritize,' she writes.

'If your boss throws fifteen things on your plate, make it a point to ask which is most important. Then, for the assignments that are absolutely a priority, do your best to not rush them. That may mean carving out extra time at home to perfect a project; homework doesn’t end when you graduate school!'

Ms Zoe also explains that her business-savvy husband, Roger Berman (pictured), is the yin to her yang. 'I bring to the table a more creative outlook and gut instinct,' she says

To rise above conflict in the workplace, Ms Zoe says: 'Kill them with kindness' until the problem dissolves 'in the form of a promotion', and to stand out in an interview, she advises: 'Go prepared'.

'Study not only the position you are applying for, but the person you are interviewing with as well.

'Have thoughtful questions ready. Be confident but not arrogant. You may even consider setting up a fake interview with your roommate or friend—it’s the ultimate way to prepare!'
