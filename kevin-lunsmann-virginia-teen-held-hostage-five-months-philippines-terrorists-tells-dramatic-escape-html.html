'I just feel grateful': Virginia teen held captive for FIVE months by Philippines terrorists known for be-heading hostages tells of dramatic escape and life back home

A Virginia teenager, held hostage by Al Qaeda inspired terrorists for five months in the Philippines, has spoken about how the ordeal still haunts him 15 months on and his dramatic escape to freedom.

Kevin Lunsmann, 14, and his mom Gerfa, of Lynchburg, Virginia, became headline news on July 11, 2011, when they were taken hostage on their summer vacation by Abu Sayyaf, an extremist group in the Philippines known for the kidnapping and beheading of Westerners.

The pair were visiting lab technician Gerfa's native country and due to return home the following day when they were kidnapped at gunpoint.

Back home: Gerfa and Kevin Lunsmann know they are lucky to be alive following their kidnap ordeal

Escape: Kevin Lunsmann, is pictured here talking to Filipino soldiers after his escape.  He today said: 'I knew it was time'.

'I wasn’t sure what was going to happen, I was just concentrating on being alive,' he told the Washington Post today. 'I was hoping that there would be some kind of rescue.'

However there wasn't.

The pair were held for two and a half months mostly in a 5-feet-by-6-feet wooden cage when Gerfa was freed.

It was another two and a half months before the teenager would make his daring escape bid. Had he been caught, he would have certainly faced death.

He told the newspaper he still has nightmares he's back in Philippines hungry, afraid and alone.

The pair were staying in accommodation on a beach in southern Tictabon Island when the kidnapping took place.

They were taken by speedboat to their prison room - not sure what would become of them.

Romnik Jakaria, 21, Kevin's cousin pushed himself on-board to support his American relatives.

'I thought it was some kind of robbery,' Gerfa told the Post. 'I thought, ‘We will run, they can have the house.’ But they surrounded us.'

'I was thinking, ‘I want to wake up. Wake up!',' Kevin said.

Adjusting: Kevin, pictured with friends in Virginia, is getting used to life back in the U.S. although he still has nightmares about his experiences

Going home: Gerfa and Kevin had returned to her home country to visit relatives. It was the day before they were due to go home when they were kidnapped

Hunt: Soldiers are pictured in the Philippines. The Lunsmanns prayed for a rescue bid but it never came

Gerfa was in her pajamas and Kevin his shorts and flip-flops. They were fed small amounts of rice and at night every fifteen minutes a torch was shone on their faces.

Courage: Kevin Lunsmann, right, with Zamboanga city mayor Celso Lobregat. Many rushed to congratulate him on his bravery

'We talked about food,' Gerfa said: 'but we tried not to.'

They told Romnik the stories of their favorite films. Avatar and Dances with Wolves were hot topics.

'We tried to talk quietly,' Kevin said, 'about things we missed, about freedom.'

“I was wondering how much longer we would be alive,' Kevin said.

The leader of the group told Gerfa he wanted $10 million and contacted her husband nursing home worker Heiko Lunsmann, 50 back in the U.S.

He was told what had happened by his wife's relatives and members of the family rushed to their congressman and the state department before the FBI arrived at his Virginia home.

Heiko spoke to the captors often in prolonged negotiations.

He feared the worst as agents overtook the family home, his heart pounding every time the phone rang.

In one night's frustration he blurted out that he didn't have the millions they wanted.

'I’m not Mel Gibson! I don’t live in Hollywood!' he shouted. 'I’m a maintenance man. I change light bulbs and unplug toilets!'

Within that first two and a half months another woman was taken hostage and freed by Filipino soldiers - the terrorists had moved the mother and son to another hut and by doing so dashed their opportunity for freedom.

When they told Gerfa she was leaving, she presumed they meant she was going to die but was surprised to be put on a boat.

A ransom had been paid - something which the family refuses to talk about.

Heiko believed they would release their son too but it wasn't to be.

 But soon his cousin was released as well.

Gerfa stayed in Manila to negotiate with the terrorists for the life of her only child while Kevin remained in the camp.

'The days just went longer and longer,' he said. 'Everything just went really slow. Everything was just silent after that.'

A police officer shows the passports of abducted U.S. citizens Gerfa Yeatts Lunsmann and her son Kevin in Zamboanga City, southern Philippines

Freedom: US teenager Kevin Eric Lunsmann talked at the city hall of Zamboanga City before he was airlifted to Manila

He saved bits and pieces he could to try to support him in his escape in a bag they had given him when moving him on. 

They included a bottle of water, a piece of rope and a few crackers

'I looked at my bag and I just felt something. It was time,' he said. 'I couldn’t stay there any longer.'

'I tried to use whatever strength I had to keep going — get as far as I could from there and then get home.'

He managed to sneak past the guards and fled to the coast overnight.

He came across farmers on the way but wasn't sure he could trust them until one approached him and asked him who he was in English.

He told him he would contact the military and then the helicopters arrived.

Kevin was fine - except for bruises on his legs and arms - and his courage was remarked upon by many.

U.S. ambassador Harry Thomas said at the time: 'In this holiday season nothing makes me happier than knowing that an innocent victim is returned to his family in time for holiday celebrations,'

'I also want to acknowledge the courage of Kevin himself, and his family, throughout this long ordeal.'

'It felt so good,' Heiko said. 'It felt good, good, good.'

'This is home. Good people, good community. This is where Kevin learned how we respect each other, how we value each other,' Gerfa said.  

'This country appreciates its people — and they will do anything to protect their people.'

Now Kevin is adapting to life back at Brookville High - skateboarding with his older half-brother, Josh Driskill and playing soccer with friends.

'I just feel grateful,' he said
